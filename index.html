Morse Code in the Civil War

PRE-MORSE CODE

The word telegraph is derived from the Greek words tele, meaning “distant,” and graphein, meaning “to write.”. Before the invention of Morse code, different forms of visual telegraphs were used over short distances.

One of the forms is a Semaphore system that used movable arms placed on a hilltop to display letters and numbers

Another is the Murray Shutter that used six octagonal that could be flipped vertically and horizontally to represent 63 distinct codes including letters and numers.

THE ELECTRIC TELEGRAPH

As the field of electricity was evolving, ideas of an electric telegraph were contemplated. Following his receiving the news that his wife had passed after she had already been buried, seeds of an electric telegraph were planted in professor Samuel Morse. During a voyage on the Sully in 1832, he gained insight into experiments with electricity and signals. This lead to the conception of the idea of an electric telegraph to which Samuel began sketching ideas of such a system. In 1835, he created a system of dots and dashes that represented numbers and letters. By 1837, Samuel had developed a form of telegraph that used electric signals to send messages and press the message on paper in terms of paper.

CIVIL WAR

The first military usage of Morse code was during the Crimean War in the 1850s. A few years later, this invention would be heavily relied upon by both the Union and Confederate troops during the civil war. The Union, with the help of the United States Military Telegraph Service(USMT), built 15000 miles of telegraph and handled about 6.5 million messages during the war. On the other hand, the Confederacy had a smaller network and used it in limited ways. This contrast was one of the factors that contributed to the Union’s victory.

BATTLES IMPACTED

Battle of Antietam

During the battle, General McClellan used the telegraph system to manage the battle’s demands by resupplying his troops with bullets and shells in the war’s bloodiest battle. Simultaneously, updates from the battle field were sent via signal flags then telegraphed directly to Washington in as little as 30 minutes allowing president Lincoln to monitor and instruct the battle in near real time. In contrast Robert E Lee’s lack of telegraphic support left them with limited intelligence and reliant on couriers which was slower than the Union’s telegraph network. Although the battle of Antietam was inconclusive, it provided president Lincoln with enough of a Union success to issue the Emancipation Proclamation shifting the focus of the war toward the abolition of slavery.

Battle of Spottylvania

Following the Battle of the Wilderness, Robert Lee repositioned his army to Spotsylvania Court House to block the Union from reaching Richmond. Under General Ulysses S. Grant, the US Military Telegraph corps (USMTC) deployed flying telegraph units. These units used horse drawn wagons with telegraph equipment and field wire which could quickly establish communication lines near battlefields. This allowed Grant to rapidly order units to support the breakthrough at Mule Shoe once the Union II corps broke through the Confederate line. In addition, Grant coordinated with General Butler on the James River and General Sherman in Georgia while the battle raged which improved the Union’s overall coordination during the war.

Battle of Gettysburg

Prior to the battle, Union sympathetic telegraph operators sent reports of the Confederate army movements which helped the Union track the general location and direction of Robert E. Lee’s army as it moved through Maryland and Pennsylvania. This awareness allowed General Meade to quickly report the situation to Henry Halleck and Abraham Lincoln in Washington which helped the coordination of reinforcement, troop movements and supplies. On the other hand, Lee’s army lacked an effective telegraph system in enemy territory which limited his ability to communicate quickly with Confederate forces thus reducing his awareness of Union positions and strategic advantages.

POST CIVIL WAR

After the American Civil War, the telegraph expanded beyond military use and became a tool for communication and business. Companies such as Western Union emerged as telegraph networks spread across the United States and internationally. Furthermore, the laying of the Transatlantic Telegraph Cable connected continents and sped up international communication. Most importantly, telegraph technology paved the way for later inventions such as the teleprinter, radio and telephone that still influence communication today
